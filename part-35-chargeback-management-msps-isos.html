Chargeback Management for MSPs and ISOs

4 min read · Part 35 of 36

Definition
A chargeback is a forced reversal of a payment transaction, initiated by a cardholder's issuing bank on the cardholder's behalf. Unlike a standard refund, which is processed voluntarily by the merchant, a chargeback bypasses the merchant entirely and returns funds directly to the cardholder through the card network. Chargebacks exist as a consumer protection mechanism and are governed by the rules of the card networks, primarily Visa and Mastercard.

How a Chargeback Differs From a Refund

The distinction between a chargeback and a refund is not merely procedural. It has meaningful consequences for the merchant.

A refund is a merchant-initiated transaction. When a customer contacts a merchant directly to report a problem, and the merchant agrees to return the funds, that refund is processed through normal payment channels. The merchant retains control over the decision, the timing, and the amount. No third party is involved.

A chargeback follows a different path entirely. The cardholder contacts their issuing bank rather than the merchant. The issuing bank reviews the claim and, if it meets the applicable criteria, initiates a reversal of the original transaction. The merchant is notified after the fact and given the opportunity to respond, but the funds are typically held or returned to the cardholder while the dispute is under review.

The financial impact of a chargeback extends beyond the transaction amount. Merchants are assessed a chargeback fee by their acquiring bank each time a chargeback is filed, regardless of the outcome. That fee is not recovered if the merchant wins the dispute. Additionally, chargebacks are recorded against the merchant's account and factor into chargeback ratio calculations, which card networks monitor closely.

So while a refund and a chargeback may produce the same result from the cardholder's perspective, they are fundamentally different events from the merchant's.

Why Chargebacks Exist

Chargebacks were introduced as a consumer protection measure. The underlying rationale is straightforward: cardholders need a mechanism to recover funds when a transaction goes wrong and the merchant either cannot or will not resolve the issue directly.

The most common scenarios that chargebacks were designed to address include unauthorized use of a payment card, merchants who fail to deliver goods or services as described, and processing errors that result in incorrect charges. In these cases, the chargeback process provides cardholders with a formal recourse path that does not require them to resolve the issue through the merchant.

This protection is a condition of card network participation. Merchants who accept Visa or Mastercard agree to the networks' dispute rules as part of their merchant agreements. That includes the chargeback process and all associated obligations.

The Difference Between a Dispute and a Chargeback

These two terms are often used interchangeably, but they refer to different stages in the same process.

A dispute is the initial claim filed by a cardholder. When a cardholder contacts their issuing bank to report a problem with a transaction, that action creates a dispute. At this stage, the issue has not yet become a formal chargeback. Depending on the card network and the nature of the claim, there may be an opportunity for the dispute to be resolved before it progresses further.

A chargeback occurs when the dispute is not resolved at the earlier stage and the issuing bank proceeds with a formal reversal. At that point, the transaction is officially classified as a chargeback, fees are assessed, and the event is recorded against the merchant's account.

The distinction matters operationally. Merchants and service providers who can identify and act on disputes before they become chargebacks have more options available to them, and fewer consequences to manage. Much of modern chargeback management strategy is built around this gap between the dispute stage and the chargeback stage.

When Chargebacks Are Legitimate

Not all chargebacks represent wrongful behavior by the cardholder. Many are the result of genuine problems: a package that never arrived, a charge that did not match the authorized amount, a subscription that was cancelled but continued to bill, or a card that was used without the cardholder's knowledge.

In these cases, the chargeback process functions as intended. The cardholder has a legitimate grievance and uses the available channel to recover their funds. Merchants who experience chargebacks of this type are often better served by addressing the underlying operational issue than by challenging the individual transaction.

When Chargebacks Are Not Legitimate

Chargebacks are not always filed in good faith. A cardholder may dispute a transaction they authorized and received in full, either because they do not recognize the charge, have forgotten the purchase, or are attempting to retain both the goods and the refund. This category of chargeback is commonly referred to as friendly fraud or first-party fraud, and it represents a growing share of overall dispute activity across many merchant categories.

From the card network's perspective, these cases are indistinguishable from legitimate disputes at the point of filing. The burden falls on the merchant to provide evidence that the transaction was authorized and fulfilled as described.

The Scope of Chargeback Liability

Merchants bear significant financial and operational exposure from chargebacks. Beyond the individual transaction amount and the associated fee, chargebacks accumulate over time and are measured as a ratio against total transaction volume. Card networks set thresholds for acceptable chargeback ratios. Merchants who exceed those thresholds can be placed into monitoring programs, face increased fees and restrictions, or in serious cases, lose the ability to accept card payments altogether.

This is why chargeback management is not simply a billing or customer service function. It is a risk management discipline that touches fraud prevention, operational processes, dispute response, and network compliance.

Chargebacks Across Card Networks

The chargeback process exists across all major card networks, but the specific rules, timelines, reason code structures, and thresholds differ between Visa and Mastercard. American Express and Discover operate their own dispute frameworks as well, though Visa and Mastercard account for the majority of card payment volume and are the primary focus of most chargeback management programs.

Understanding which network governs a given transaction is relevant because it determines which rules apply, what evidence is required, and what deadlines must be met.
